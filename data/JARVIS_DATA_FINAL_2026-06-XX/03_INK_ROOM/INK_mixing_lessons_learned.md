# Ink Mixing Lessons Learned

## Purpose

This file contains practical ink-room lessons learned from recent ink-mixing work, drawdowns, press corrections, mistakes, and successful adjustments.

This file is **guidance only**. It should help JARVIS explain good ink-mixing practice, suggest cautious next steps, and remind coworkers what to check. It is **not automatic approval** for production color.

Final color approval must still come from a qualified human, press-side review, customer requirements, or vendor support when needed.

---

## Core Ink Matching Principles

### Match the real job, not just the book

A Pantone book chip is the target reference, but the real result depends on:

- envelope stock
- paper color
- paper absorbency
- woven/laid texture
- flood versus spot coverage
- anilox/film thickness
- press speed
- dryback
- lighting
- ink strength
- extender level

A drawdown can look acceptable while the press result prints too light, too strong, or shifted.

### Start with the closest known good source

Best starting points, in order:

1. A vendor-supplied ink that already ran correctly.
2. A proven internal formula that has run on the same substrate and coverage type.
3. A small test batch based on the Pantone book formula.
4. A cautious scratch formula built from known colorants.

Do not jump from an unproven formula straight to a large production batch.

### Make small controlled changes

When correcting ink, change **one variable at a time** whenever possible.

Good correction process:

1. Add one ingredient.
2. Record the exact weight.
3. Mix thoroughly.
4. Pull a drawdown.
5. Let it dry.
6. Compare under good light.
7. Decide next move.

Changing multiple ingredients at once makes it harder to know what fixed or ruined the color.

### Do not chase wet ink

Wet ink can look stronger, glossier, darker, or different in hue than dry ink. Always let the drawdown dry enough before making final judgment.

### Strength and hue are different problems

Before adding anything, decide which problem you are fixing:

- **Strength problem:** too strong, too weak, too dark, too light, too transparent.
- **Hue problem:** too red, too yellow, too blue, too green, too purple, too orange.
- **Cleanliness problem:** too muddy, dirty, dull, brown, gray, or lifeless.

Adding extender helps strength, but it may not fix hue. Adding pigment helps hue or strength, but can easily create a new problem.

### Press operators can usually lighten ink easier than darken it

For many jobs, especially flood coats, press operators are used to adding extender to lighten ink. They cannot easily make a weak ink stronger unless they have the right pigment or base available.

This does **not** mean every flood should be extremely strong. It means weak ink is often harder to fix at press than slightly strong ink.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

### Adding too much pigment too quickly

Small pigment additions can make a large change, especially with:

- black
- blue
- violet
- rubine
- strong red
- process yellow in pale yellows

Do not make large pigment additions unless the batch is clearly far off and the direction is obvious.

### Trusting old formulas without question

Old formulas may not match current ink systems, current pigments, current substrates, or current press conditions. A formula with no extender, very low extender, or strange colorants should be treated as suspicious until tested.

### Assuming a Pantone book formula will run directly on press

Pantone book formulas are useful references, but production ink may need adjustment for:

- flexo printing
- envelope paper
- flood coat coverage
- actual ink strength
- vendor colorant differences

### Adding black too early

Black can help mute or deepen a color, but it can also make the ink dirty, gray, brown, or lifeless. Black is hard to recover from once overused.

Use black as a final fine correction, not as the first answer unless the target clearly requires it.

### Using extender to fix a hue problem

If the color is too red, too blue, too yellow, or too purple, extender may only make a lighter wrong color. Extender lowers strength. It does not truly change the color direction.

### Chasing color under poor lighting

Color should be checked under consistent lighting. Poor lighting can make an acceptable color look wrong or a wrong color look close.

The INX color viewing card/light indicator is useful because it isolates color and warns if the light is not close to 5000K.

### Not recording changes

If additions are made by eye and not recorded, the team may get the color right once but fail to reproduce it later.

Record:

- starting formula
- batch size
- each added ingredient
- each added weight
- drawdown result
- press result
- final accepted formula

### Correcting from memory instead of the current batch

Always know what is currently in the bucket. If several corrections have been made, add up the total weights before deciding the next step.

### Scaling before testing

Do not scale a brand-new formula to 100, 200, 300, or 400 pounds until a small test batch has been proven with a drawdown and, ideally, a press check.

## Drawdown Lessons

### Drawdowns are necessary, but not perfect

A drawdown is the first checkpoint. It helps compare color before going to press, but it is not identical to press conditions.

A drawdown may differ because of:

- heavier film thickness
- different pressure from the drawdown tool
- smoother paper than the envelope stock
- wet versus dry appearance
- gloss/sheen difference
- absorbency difference
- texture difference

### Always compare on the correct substrate when possible

If the job prints on white woven envelope stock, test on white woven stock when available. A drawdown on plain white paper can mislead.

### Let the drawdown dry

Do not judge a wet drawdown as final. Let it dry enough to see the real color and dryback.

### Use a viewing mask

A black color viewing mask helps isolate the color and prevents surrounding colors from tricking the eye. The mask should be matte black or neutral dark, not glossy, purple-black, or white.

### Check film thickness

If the drawdown is heavy, it may look stronger than the press result. This matters especially with flood coats.

### Good enough depends on the job

Some colors can be press-approved when visually close. Others are customer-critical and need stricter control.

When unsure, escalate.

## Correction Strategies

Use this logic as guidance, not automatic approval.

### If ink is too light or weak

Possible actions:

- Add the main colorant or base color.
- For a known formula, add colorants in the same ratio as the formula.
- For flood coats, consider whether the press film is thinner than the drawdown.

Avoid adding black just to make a color stronger unless the target also needs muting.

### If ink is too dark or too strong

Possible actions:

- Add extender.
- Add extender in a controlled amount, mix, dry drawdown, and compare.
- If the hue is right but strength is high, extender is usually correct.

### If ink is too red

Possible actions depend on target:

- Add extender if it is simply too strong.
- Add a small amount of blue/violet only if it needs to cool.
- Add yellow only if it needs to warm toward orange/peach, not if it is already warm.

### If ink is too yellow

Possible actions:

- Add a tiny amount of the opposing/cooling color if appropriate.
- Add black only if the target is muted/olive/brown and needs dulling.
- For pale yellows, avoid overcorrecting with red or black.

### If ink is too blue or cyan

Possible actions:

- Add violet if the target needs a deeper blue-violet tone.
- Add extender if it is too strong.
- Add a tiny amount of black only if it is too bright/electric and needs muting.
- Avoid black if the color needs to stay clean.

### If ink is too purple

Possible actions:

- Add the main blue if it should be blue.
- Add extender if it is too strong.
- Avoid more violet.
- Avoid black unless the target is supposed to be muted.

### If ink is too orange or coral

Possible actions:

- Add extender first if it is too strong.
- Add a tiny amount of blue only if the strength is close and the hue needs cooling.
- Avoid yellow if it already looks too warm.

### If ink is too muddy, dirty, brown, or gray

Possible actions:

- Stop adding black or dirtying pigments.
- Consider abandoning the batch if it is badly contaminated.
- If salvage is possible, cut with extender and rebuild the clean color carefully.
- If the job is customer-critical, ask for help.

### If ink is too transparent

Possible actions:

- Check if opacity is actually required or if the color is intentionally transparent.
- Reduce extender only after confirming the target should print stronger.
- Add main colorant carefully.
- Consider vendor support if opacity is a recurring issue.

### If ink is too strong but still wrong in hue

Do not keep adding extender forever if the hue is wrong. Extender may only create a lighter wrong color. Fix the hue after strength is close.

## Extender Lessons

### What extender does

Extender generally:

- lowers color strength
- increases transparency
- lightens the apparent color
- can reduce saturation
- can help press operators fine-tune strong ink
- may change viscosity/handling depending on the ink system

### What extender does not do

Extender does not truly fix hue. If the ink is too coral, too purple, too green, or too dirty, extender may only make a paler version of the same problem.

### Extender in pale colors

Very pale colors can legitimately require high extender percentages. Examples observed in this work included pale yellows and pale pinks where the final direction was mostly extender.

### Extender in flood coats

Flood coats are tricky. Some flood inks printed too light on press when the drawdown looked acceptable. However, not every flood should be made aggressively strong.

Lesson: flood coat strength must be judged by press behavior, not by a single rule.

### When extender barely changes the color

If a large extender addition barely changes the visual result, the issue may be:

- wrong hue
- too much strong pigment still dominating
- heavy drawdown film
- poor comparison conditions
- inadequate mixing
- press/drawdown mismatch

At that point, stop and reassess before adding more.

## Pigment / Colorant Lessons

These are shop observations from recent mixing work, not universal chemical rules.

### Red 22 / Red 23

Red 22/23 can build red and pink strength quickly. In pale colors, even small overages can make the ink too hot, coral, or saturated.

For pale pinks, red-only formulas can be cleaner than adding multiple correction colors too early.

### Rubine Red

Rubine can help make pinks more vibrant or rose/magenta. It can also push colors too pink or too hot if overused.

Use Rubine when a red needs more pink/rose character, not when the color is already too magenta.

### Warm Red

Some old formulas used Warm Red. If Warm Red is no longer used in the current system, do not trust old formulas blindly. Convert cautiously using available red colorants and test small.

### 23 Yellow 14

23 Yellow 14 tends to be warmer/oranger than process yellow. It can help add body to yellows and oranges, but it can make pale yellows too warm or dirty if overused.

### Process Yellow

Process Yellow is cleaner and better for very pale clean yellows. It was useful for light yellow work where 23 Yellow 14 would have been too warm.

### Blue 15:3

Blue 15:3 is strong and useful for cyan/blue corrections. It can quickly build strength. In some blue formulas, too much blue or too much accompanying violet can push the color toward 300-style deep blue instead of lighter 299-style blue.

### Methyl Violet / Violet

Violet is powerful. It can deepen blue and purple formulas, but it can make colors purple fast. Use small additions.

For blue formulas, violet can replace some of the effect of Reflex Blue or Violet V2, but it must be controlled.

### Eclipse Black / Process Black

Black can mute, deepen, and dirty colors. It is useful in muted olives, dusty reds, or some deep colors, but it caused problems when used to correct a clean blue.

Avoid black in clean bright colors unless the drawdown proves it is needed.

### Extender

Extender is not just “filler.” It is a major part of the formula and controls strength, transparency, and press behavior. Record extender just as carefully as pigment.

### Vendor premade ink

Vendor-supplied ink should be treated as a valuable reference. If INX premade ink runs correctly, match future internal formulas to the successful bucket sample or press-approved adjusted sample.

## Formula Building Workflow

1. **Identify target color and substrate.**  
   Confirm Pantone number, U/C version, envelope stock, flood/spot use, and customer requirements.

2. **Start with closest known base/formula.**  
   Use a proven vendor ink, prior accepted formula, or closest successful shop formula.

3. **Make a small test batch.**  
   Avoid large batches until the small batch is close. Use 5, 10, 15, or 20 pounds depending on the job and scale accuracy.

4. **Record exact weights.**  
   Record every ingredient and actual weight, even if you overshoot.

5. **Pull drawdown.**  
   Use consistent method, pressure, and substrate when possible.

6. **Let it dry.**  
   Do not judge only from wet ink.

7. **Compare under good lighting.**  
   Use the Pantone chip, actual job stock, and color viewing mask when possible.

8. **Adjust one variable at a time.**  
   Decide whether the issue is strength, hue, or cleanliness before adding anything.

9. **Record every change.**  
   Add corrections to the batch math immediately.

10. **Do not scale up until the small batch is acceptable.**  
    Scale only a proven formula using percentages.

11. **Press-test if the job is important.**  
    Flood coats and customer-critical colors should be checked on press before making large amounts.

12. **Save the final formula with notes.**  
    Include whether it was drawdown-only, press-approved, vendor-matched, flood-specific, or spot-specific.

## Scaling Lessons

### Percentages matter

Always convert the final accepted formula to percentages before scaling. Do not scale from rounded weights unless necessary.

### Batch size changes precision

Tiny colorants may be hard to weigh accurately in small batches. A 0.03 lb addition may be meaningful but hard to control. Larger test batches may be more accurate when using small strong pigments.

### Rounding can shift color

For small batches, rounding can noticeably change the formula. Record actual weights, not intended weights.

### Large batch scaling needs confidence

Do not scale a guessed or untested formula to 200, 300, or 400 pounds. A small mistake becomes expensive fast.

### Example scaling logic

If a formula is:

- Extender = 80.52%
- Blue = 17.97%
- Violet = 1.51%

Then for a target batch:

- Extender pounds = target pounds × 0.8052
- Blue pounds = target pounds × 0.1797
- Violet pounds = target pounds × 0.0151

Always verify the final total.

## Press Reality Checks

### Flood coats behave differently

Flood coats have repeatedly been the hardest to predict. A drawdown may look strong enough while the press spreads the ink thinner and the color prints too light.

### But not every flood needs to be heavily strengthened

The 510 U flood work showed that very pale colors can still be ruined by making them too strong. The better lesson is:

**Flood coats require press-aware testing, not blind strength increases.**

### Press operators have useful practical knowledge

Operators may know that a job usually needs ink darker than expected or that extender is normally added at press. Listen to that input, but still record actual changes.

### Press-side changes must be recorded

If press adds pigment or extender by eye and the color works, try to document what was added. Otherwise the successful correction cannot be reproduced.

### When to press-test

Press-test before large-scale production when:

- it is a flood coat
- the color is customer-critical
- the formula is new
- the formula was heavily corrected
- the batch is large
- previous attempts failed
- vendor ink is being matched

## When To Stop And Ask For Help

JARVIS should recommend asking Jonathan, INX, Ted Carter, or another qualified person when:

- the batch is for a live production run
- the batch is large or expensive
- the color is customer-critical
- the ink has been corrected multiple times and is still wrong
- black or several pigments have dirtied the batch
- the user does not know what has already been added
- the formula is old, untrusted, or missing ingredients
- the request involves replacing or extending vendor-supplied ink
- the job is a flood coat and the drawdown/press result disagrees
- there is uncertainty about pigment names or substitutions
- a coworker wants JARVIS to approve the final color

When in doubt, JARVIS should escalate instead of guessing.

## Quick Reference

### Before mixing

- Confirm Pantone number and U/C version.
- Confirm spot color or flood coat.
- Confirm substrate.
- Look for a proven formula first.
- Check whether vendor ink exists.
- Start small unless formula is already proven.

### During mixing

- Weigh accurately.
- Record actual weights.
- Mix thoroughly.
- Pull drawdown.
- Let dry.
- Compare under good light.
- Use a viewing mask.
- Change one thing at a time.

### If color is wrong

- Too strong/dark: add extender.
- Too weak/light: add main colorant or base ratio.
- Too dirty/muddy: stop adding black/dirtying pigments; reassess.
- Too warm/coral: consider tiny blue only after strength is close.
- Too cool/purple: consider red/yellow balance, but avoid large moves.
- Too yellow/bright: mute carefully; black only if target is muted.
- Too blue/bright: violet may deepen; black only as a tiny final mute.
- Still wrong after several corrections: stop and ask for help.

### Before scaling

- Convert final batch to percentages.
- Confirm total equals 100%.
- Confirm whether formula is drawdown-only or press-approved.
- Do not scale an experimental formula to a large batch without review.

### Final reminder

Ink matching is practical craft work. JARVIS can help with math, memory, and cautious troubleshooting, but a human must verify the actual color.
